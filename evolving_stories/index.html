evolving stories: notes and experiments

A hand-cranked, lazy notebook of sorts, designed to capture my thoughts about comics over the summer.

20 July 2018

Context and background

A few months ago, I looked at characters bound by narrative rules and asked artists to contribute a comic following those rules. How else can rules be used in comics? Conway's Game of Life evolves based on an initial configuration. Comics are written and controlled by the author/artist, but there still could be something in a game-like approach to narrative storytelling.

I'd like to explore a more experimental and potentially more abstract approach to sequential storytelling. I'm interested in time, rules, and minimalism. A couple of artists who have looked at this already, apart from Richard McGuire in Here: these are Nadine Redlich's Ambient Comics explore tiny moments in time: a train passing some mountains, the striking of a match, etc. They are basically sentences, rather than novels.

Another artist experimenting with short moments is Marc-Antoine Mathieu, who managed to product almost 70 pages from a time period of 3 seconds in 3".

Themes

  1. The natural world, evolution
  2. Time and transition
  3. Minimalism

Known limitations

  1. Silence: visual communication alone
  2. Short: poetry/paragraph length comics, rather than graphic novel length – each comic could be a unit making up a larger whole, similar to an anthology or series
  3. Format will be a web comic

Ideas

In no particular order

  • grids
    Comics are stories sequenced through panels in a grid. How can I experiment with the grid? What about transitions and movement within a larger image? A static central image and elements around it changing and moving? Similar to Tudor paintings and older sequential art. Time can work at a different pace within the same larger image (eg: the camera focuses on some hills, but the sun is rising and setting, a bird is flying past...)

  • small events within a single frame
    a window overlooking a garden, in which something small and new happens every day

  • evolution visualised
    evolution takes place over millions of years: a cross section of evolutionary history, a million years per panel, focusing on plant / animal life on earth

  • obfuscated super-minimalist storytelling
    i. Miniature, minimalist stories: the smallest possible unit for storytelling. A previous idea was to draw comics so small that you need a magnifying glass to look at them with.
    ii. Visually oblique and minimalist stories: abstracted, but still communicating the story of something. What's the most basic way to represent a character or a scene?
