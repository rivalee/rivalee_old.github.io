evolving stories: notes and experiments

A hand-cranked, lazy notebook of sorts, designed to capture my thoughts about comics over the summer.

22 July 2018

Settling on an idea

I'm going to start by exploring two ideas: grids and events happening within a single frame / scene. These feel small and self-contained, and have mileage to be developed into a series of drawings.

20 July 2018

Context and background

A few months ago, I looked at characters bound by narrative rules and asked artists to contribute a comic following those rules. How else can rules be used in comics? Conway's Game of Life evolves based on an initial configuration. Comics are written and controlled by the author/artist, but there still could be something in a game-like approach to narrative storytelling.

I'd like to explore a more experimental and potentially more abstract approach to sequential storytelling. I'm interested in time, rules, and minimalism. A couple of artists who have looked at this already, apart from Richard McGuire in Here: these are Nadine Redlich's Ambient Comics explore tiny moments in time: a train passing some mountains, the striking of a match, etc. They are basically sentences, rather than novels.

Another artist experimenting with short moments is Marc-Antoine Mathieu, who managed to produce almost 70 pages from a time period of 3 seconds in 3".

Themes

  1. The natural world, evolution
  2. Time and transition
  3. Minimalism

Known limitations

  1. Silence: visual communication alone
  2. Short: poetry/paragraph length comics, rather than graphic novel length – each comic could be a unit making up a larger whole, similar to an anthology or series
  3. Format will be a web comic

Ideas

In no particular order / most of these ideas are about how form relates to storytelling

  • grids
    i. Comics are stories sequenced through panels in a grid. Do grids need to be sequential?
    ii. Grids are also ways to structure content: they support patterns and can show transition and change. Could grids be an interesting way to show transitions and movement within a larger image?

  • events happening within a single frame / scene
    The frame is like a stage in which different events occur.
    i. a window overlooking a scene, in which something small and new happens every day
    ii. a simple idea: overgrown: a garden slowly becomes overgrown over a period of time, falling into disrepair (and is discovered again?)
    iii. a more complex idea: every object in the scene has a story (mini-comic) attached to it

  • evolution
    i. takes place over millions of years: interesting time frame to experiment with and visualise
    ii. a cross section of evolutionary history, a million years per panel, focusing on plant / animal life on earth
    ii. extrapolate evolution: demonstrate it through imagery without doing a literal interpretation (like Conway's Game of Life)

  • obfuscated super-minimalist storytelling
    i. what's the smallest possible unit for storytelling?
    ii. oblique and minimalist stories: abstracted, but still communicating the story of something. What's the most basic way to represent a character or a scene?
